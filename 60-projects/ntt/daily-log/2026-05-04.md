## Stage 1 — Initial framing — "this is a bomb"

Victor's verbal description of the incoming change (paraphrased):

> "Currently Dynatrace is monitoring the Cisco and Fortinet estates, raising INCs in ServiceNow. ServiceNow ingests CIs related classes via three pipes: manual, Cisco DNA and Dynatrace. But now — I'm reading SOW that came from NTT who will support this entire infrastructure through their own monitoring tool (whatever that is) and their ServiceNow platform. They need to integrate their platform with ours. If that is not a bomb, I don't know what is?"

### My initial breakdown — five axes of complication

Without seeing the SOW yet, I named the dimensions of the integration problem assuming a true outsourced-with-own-stack model:

1. **Two ServiceNow instances now in scope.** SN-to-SN integration is its own discipline — CMDB synchronisation, incident exchange, SLA boundary enforcement. Decisions inherited before any technical work: which side authoritative for which CI classes, which side owns which incidents, how cross-instance updates round-trip, what happens when one side closes an incident the other still has open.

2. **Monitoring overlap or replacement.** "NTT's own monitoring tool" against existing Dynatrace creates one of three states — coexistence (duplicate signals, dedup rules), replacement-for-NTT-scope (split-brain by component), full replacement (Dynatrace decommissioned for infra). Each path has different consequences for the SN incident-routing objective.

3. **CI ingestion topology becomes ≥ 4 pipes.** Manual + Cisco DNA + Dynatrace + NTT's CMDB feed (or some bridge from it). With four pipes you genuinely need an authoritative-source-per-class matrix or you'll get conflicting CIs.

4. **Operational ownership boundary.** "NTT supports the infrastructure" means they create/own infra-tier incidents in their SN; CCH SN holds business-impact / service-tier context. Escalation path between the two is the load-bearing piece — and exactly where CSDM v5's BSO/TSO Parent-Child pattern earns its keep, except now Parent and Child can be in *different ServiceNow instances*. File 04 doesn't currently address that.

5. **Direct intersection with today's SAP work.** "ServiceNow properly raises and routes SAP incidents" objective now has a possible third dimension — SAP infra alerts may flow through NTT's SN before reaching CCH SN, depending on where the boundary lands. The B1 fix (alerting profile on PRD_S4HANA) remains the right immediate move, but routing target may end up being NTT's integration channel, not direct-to-CCH-SN.

### My response — yes, that is the standard managed-service pattern

Validated the reframing as the **managed-service integration pattern**, mapping Victor's instinct onto the established model:

| Concern | Direction | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Infra CIs | NTT → CCH (single pipe) | NTT CMDB authoritative; CCH imports as reference |
| Infra incidents | NTT → CCH (FYI) | CCH read-only awareness for business-impact correlation |
| Problems CCH originates against infra | CCH → NTT | Outbound e-bond — capacity, anomalies the app teams notice |
| Changes CCH originates against infra | CCH → NTT | Outbound e-bond — RITM/CHG flow |
| Application incidents | CCH → CCH (internal) | App teams own; correlated upward to CCH BSOs |

Caveats raised:

- **Dynatrace doesn't disappear — it shifts scope.** NTT replaces Cisco/Fortinet *infra* monitoring. Dynatrace stays for the **application tier** — SAP especially. Today's SAP work on files 02/04 remains valid; sits inside CCH's retained scope, with NTT-sourced infra CIs as upstream references in the CSDM graph.
- **CSDM v5 cross-boundary relationships need a design call once.** When NTT infra CI fails and FYI lands in CCH SN, value of correlating with a CCH-side SAP application incident depends on `App Service → Infra CI` relationship being present. If NTT's pipe imports CIs *with their relationships to CCH-side services*, you get auto-correlation. Worth flagging to NTT in integration design — easier to set up once than to retrofit.

## Stage 5 — Victor's confusion — "but they still want INC and CHG e-bond"

Victor pushed back:

> "Why then they requested INC and Change e-bond to our platform? I'm still confused. Everything you say — I understand but then there are other developments aside that make me confused."

### My response — the two-layer ITSM reality

Both can be true simultaneously. When you have a managed-service relationship with a global supplier, there are two distinct operational realities:

| Layer | Whose SN? | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer-facing** | CCH's ServiceNow | Source of truth for CCH's view — incidents users see, CMDB, business-impact correlation, SLA reporting to CCH leadership |
| **Supplier-internal** | Supplier's own ServiceNow (NTT runs one for global ops) | Where their engineers pick up work, where on-call paging triggers, where they track work against their internal SLAs, where OEM escalations route, where multi-client portfolio reporting lives |

A global supplier *must* have their own ITSM platform. They can't run global operations from inside each customer's tenant — they have hundreds of customers. Their engineers' workdays happen in their own SN, not the customer's.

So:
- **Section 2.6.7** ("use CCH's ServiceNow") = the *customer-facing* tickets live in CCH SN. From CCH's perspective, the ticket of record is in CCH SN.
- **E-bond INC + CHG** = synchronisation between CCH SN and supplier SN so both layers stay consistent. The same ticket exists in both systems, status/comments/attachments propagate, but each platform is source of truth for its own audience.

### Why specifically INC and CHG e-bond

| Direction | Pattern |
|---|---|
| **INC: CCH → NTT** | A user reports an issue in CCH SN; NTT engineer needs it in their queue / paging system / on-call routing. NTT SN copy created via e-bond. Engineer works in NTT SN; updates flow back. |
| **INC: NTT → CCH** | Their AIOps tool generates an automated incident in NTT SN; needs to surface to CCH for visibility + SLA tracking. E-bond pushes a copy to CCH SN. |
| **CHG: NTT → CCH** | The load-bearing one. NTT engineer plans a firewall rule change. Originates in NTT's CHG system. CCH owns the CAB approval and risk acceptance for changes against CCH infrastructure. So the CHG **must** appear in CCH SN for CAB review, approval gates, and audit trail. NTT executes only after approval; status syncs back. |
| **CHG: CCH → NTT** | A CCH application team initiates a change request that requires NTT to do the work (firewall rule, network reconfiguration). RITM/CHG opens in CCH SN, e-bonds to NTT for execution. |

### The clean mental model

**One source of truth per audience.** CCH's view is authoritative in CCH SN. NTT's view is authoritative in NTT SN. E-bond is the synchronisation glue. Neither is a "copy" of the other — they're two faithful representations of the same operational reality, each curated for its audience.

The CHG case is why e-bond isn't optional in this contract — CCH retains ownership of the change-approval authority. Even though NTT does the operational work, CCH's CAB has to approve any change touching CCH infrastructure. That's a control CCH cannot give up. E-bond is the mechanism that makes this work without forcing NTT engineers to live inside CCH SN for change planning.

## Stage 9 — NTT DATA SOW Attachments H–N captured

### Attachment H — Access Control and Authentication Management

**H.1 Access Management for Service operations:**
- CCH provisions credentials for designated NTT personnel via existing CCH access mechanisms.
- NTT retains **exclusive administrative rights** over managed items by default.
- Two exception paths:
  - **Emergency Access**: CCH gets admin account, used **only** if NTT cannot deliver due to Force Majeure.
  - **Shared Management**: NTT temporarily grants admin to CCH or third party for specific business needs.
- During any shared/emergency access window:
  1. NTT SLA targets are **suspended**.
  2. Monitoring alerts may be disabled to prevent CCH/third-party-caused false positives.
  3. NTT troubleshooting of CCH-caused issues = **chargeable**.
  4. Post-access **Network Health Assessment** may be conducted (chargeable); NTT reserves the right to **reverse Client/third-party changes** if needed for supportability.

This dovetails with C.9 (co-management read-only model). Together they create a strong financial disincentive against CCH using break-glass access casually — every use costs SLA suspension + potential cleanup billing.

**H.2 PAM (Privileged Access Management) — important model clarification:**

> "CCH will use its own Privileged Access Management (PAM) solution for all named administrative accounts created for NTT DATA personnel."

This is the **"Client PAM Named Accounts"** model under NTT's standard managed-network-service documentation. Key implications:

- **CCH operates the PAM platform** — provisions, rotates, revokes named accounts; enforces auth/authz/session control; maintains access logs and audit trails.
- **NTT does NOT manage CCH's PAM** under this SOW.
- **NTT keeps its own operational logs in parallel** — incidents, changes, configuration updates, device access traces — independent from CCH PAM logs. Both sets of logs may be referenced for service reporting, troubleshooting, compliance reviews, RCA.

**Practical consequence**: CCH retains the kill-switch for NTT access. If a relationship souring or a security incident requires immediate revocation, CCH does it on the PAM side, no NTT cooperation needed.

**H.3 Remote Access / Connectivity:**
- CCH provides: VPN connectivity, IP addresses, firewall ports, S2S VPN endpoint, IPSEC encryption per Appendix 2, equipment credentials (usernames, passwords, API tokens, community strings).
- NTT provides: their **Connectivity Platform** (S2S VPN endpoint), IP addressing, encryption keys/certs, SNMP/API config requirements.
- So NTT *does* have some infrastructure of their own — the Connectivity Platform endpoint that bridges into CCH's network. This is the rails the operational integration runs on.

### Attachment I — Service Level Targets (and the enforceability tension)

The actual SLA target tables (I.1–I.7: notification, response, restoration, request response/fulfilment, RCA, SDS availability) were not visible in the shared content — likely embedded as tables/images. Worth following up on next session.

What WAS visible is the **enforceability framing** (load-bearing legal language):

> "NTT DATA will use **commercially reasonable efforts** to meet the SLAs specified below. For the avoidance of doubt, the parties hereby expressly acknowledge and agree that **any failure on the part of NTT DATA to satisfy the SLAs will not give rise to any liability** and will not be used for purposes of termination or entitle Client to claim any credit, remediation, or any other remedy."

Then **I.8 — Service Credit** defines a **3-tier model** that *does* provide remediation:

- **Measurement interval**: monthly.
- **Trigger**: service credits payable starting from the **third occurrence** of failure for the same service within any 12-month period (or second consecutive occurrence per Example B).
- **Calculation**: Monthly At Risk Amount = **15% of Monthly Fee** for the affected service.
- **Total credit**: sum of the Monthly At Risk Amounts for each month with qualifying breaches.

Three illustrative examples:
- A) Three non-consecutive months × 15% × €50k = €22.5k credit.
- B) Two consecutive months × 15% × €50k = €15k credit (if "two consecutive" interpretation applies).
- C) Three non-consecutive months with variable monthly fees = €21k credit.

**The tension**: the opening says "no liability, no remedy"; I.8 then defines remedies. Reconciliation: the **service credits are voluntarily provided per the I.8 mechanism, but are NOT contractual obligations or breach remedies**. They're a goodwill/commercial concession, not an enforceable SLA penalty. CCH cannot terminate for SLA breach and cannot claim any remedy beyond the I.8 credits (and even those are framed as discretionary in the opening clause).

**Practical consequence**: CCH operates with a **soft SLA regime**. NTT commits to "commercially reasonable efforts" + capped service credits after multiple failures. There is no hard contractual lever (termination, damages) tied to SLA performance. Worth flagging in any pre-signature legal review.
